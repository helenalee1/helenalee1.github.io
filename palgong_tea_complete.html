01

The brief — and what it wasn't

Context

Palgong Tea's Steeles location had just re-opened after a long hiatus and was introducing something new: self-serve ramen machines, inspired by the viral Han River ramen trend from Korea. The concept — customers selecting and preparing their own instant ramen, alongside Palgong's signature bubble teas — was already a cultural moment in Seoul, spreading globally through K-entertainment.

For the Maizon take-home, I was given the concept and nothing else. My task was to figure out what the opportunity was, prove it with research, and build a full strategy to go after it.

"The absence of a brief was the brief — the assignment tested whether I could build structure from ambiguity."
Starting point: The @palgongtea.steeles account had under 100 followers. Any strategy had to begin with organic community-building — meaning sequencing mattered as much as the ideas themselves.

06

What I pushed back on

Brand thinking

Rather than just deliver a strategy that said yes to everything, I included a section challenging assumptions about the collaboration — because brand consistency is a real risk a good marketing strategy shouldn't ignore.

Brand risks I flagged
1
Ambience tension: Palgong Tea built its reputation on calm, inviting spaces for tea and study. Aromatic instant ramen could subtly shift the store's atmosphere and confuse regular visitors.
2
My recommendation: Position it as a location-exclusive feature or limited-time cultural event — not a permanent menu change. Protects the parent brand while giving Steeles permission to experiment.
3
The upside case: K-culture's global momentum creates a window that won't always be open — making this a bold but defensible opportunity if scoped correctly.
"The best strategy isn't the one that says yes to everything — it's the one that earns its yes by naming the tradeoffs first."
07

What I learned

Reflection
1
Audience sequencing matters as much as audience targeting
Starting from near-zero meant I couldn't just build a campaign — I had to build an audience first. Phase 1 existed entirely to earn the right to run Phase 2.
2
Cultural resonance has to be earned, not borrowed
The K-culture angle worked because the product genuinely originated from that context. Manufactured cultural relevance tends to backfire; real alignment compounds.
3
A good brief includes the questions it doesn't answer
Working without a brief forced me to write one — to define what success meant, what constraints existed, and what tradeoffs the brand should consider. That process taught me more about strategic framing than any guided assignment would have.

01

The challenge: an unexplained spike

Context

CIBC's Investor's Edge campaign, promoting an online and mobile trading platform for smarter investment decisions, had historically struggled with engagement. For most runs, offer acceptance rates sat below 20%, raising concerns among senior leadership about the program's long-term viability.

Then the September campaign produced a sudden, significant surge in acceptance rates. No one knew exactly why. My job was to find out and turn that answer into a repeatable strategy.

The question I was trying to solve: Was this a fluke, or had something genuinely changed? And if it had changed, could we do it again intentionally?
The three questions I asked myself
?
Was low engagement driven by offer irrelevance, targeting issues, or timing?
Previous campaign runs varied in offer type and audience segment. I needed to isolate which variable was actually correlated with drop-off before building any recommendation.
?
What changed in September, and could it be replicated?
A performance anomaly is only useful if you can explain it. The goal was to move from "this happened" to "this happened because of X", so the team could design for it rather than hope for it.
?
What strategic roadmap would sustain improved performance?
Even if the drivers were identified, the team needed a clear path forward and a sequenced plan with both quick wins and longer-term structural changes.

03

Three recommendations and adoption

Strategic thinking

I developed three distinct data-driven recommendations to explain the engagement boost and show what would guarantee success with future Investor's Edge campaigns, giving the team multiple levers to test and optimize independently. All three were validated by cross-functional stakeholders and adopted for future campaign execution.

R1
Pre-priming through educational content increases offer receptiveness
Customers who received prior feature announcements and educational touchpoints were more familiar with Investor's Edge, reducing friction at the offer stage. Familiarity drove acceptance, not the offer itself.
✓ Adopted
R2
Lower wallet threshold offers improved first-time user conversion
Investing is unfamiliar and scary to a lot of people. Historical offers may have been misaligned with where first-time users were in their investment journey. Lower entry thresholds reduced perceived risk and made the offer relevant to a broader segment of the audience.
✓ Adopted
R3
Message alignment with first-time users drove September's lift
The September campaign's messaging was better calibrated for clients earlier in their investment journey, speaking to exploration and learning rather than advanced trading. Audience-message fit was the differentiator and simplified storytelling is key for investing campaigns.
✓ Adopted
Strategic roadmap I built
Quick wins (immediate)
Pre-campaign warm-up sequence
·Introduce educational touchpoints 4–6 weeks before offer delivery
·Segment audience by prior engagement with feature content
·Adjust offer threshold for first-time investor segments
Long-term (structural)
Always-on audience nurture program
·Build a continuous education track for pre-campaign audience priming
·Test message variants calibrated to investor journey stage
·Implement Tableau-based performance tracking for real-time optimization

1
The most valuable analysis is the one that changes a decision
The goal of my analysis was never to produce a report — it was to shift how the team made future decisions. When all 3 recommendations were adopted, it confirmed that the work had succeeded on that measure. Insights that sit in a deck and don't influence execution are a wasted cycle.
2
Anomalies are the most valuable data points — if you treat them as questions
Most teams see a performance spike and move on. Treating the September surge as a question — "why did this happen and can we make it happen again?" — turned a lucky result into a strategic asset. The instinct to investigate outliers rather than dismiss them is something I now carry into every project.
3
Presenting to senior audiences requires a different kind of structure
A 100-person all-hands with senior leadership isn't the same as presenting to your manager. I learned to lead with the so-what — the strategic implication — before the how. Data is evidence; the recommendation is the point. Leading with the insight, not the methodology, is what gets a room to move.
4
Automation is a form of strategic contribution
Building the Tableau reporting system wasn't just an efficiency play — it changed what the team could do. When leadership can access 30+ insights on demand without waiting for manual compilation, the pace of decision-making changes. Good tooling is a form of influence that outlasts your tenure.
